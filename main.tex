\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Modern Internet of Things (IoT) systems generate continuous streams of sensor data throughout their operational life, yet deciding exactly when to perform maintenance remains a difficult problem. Scheduling maintenance too early wastes useful asset life and incurs unnecessary cost, while acting too late risks catastrophic failure. The challenge is compounded by the reality that sensors degrade over time, injecting noise into the very data that maintenance decisions depend on. Most existing predictive maintenance systems treat their own predictions as ground truth, leaving them vulnerable to silent failures when sensor quality deteriorates.

The work reported here addressed that gap by developing a three-layer hybrid artificial intelligence framework that combines deep learning, reinforcement learning, and deterministic safety logic for IoT-based predictive maintenance scheduling. The framework is asset-agnostic in interface design; turbofan engines from the NASA C-MAPSS dataset were used as a demanding, safety-critical benchmark. Layer~1 employed a deep ensemble of five Long Short-Term Memory networks, each trained on a different random 80\% subset of the C-MAPSS data (FD001 and FD002, 360 engines combined). The ensemble predicted Remaining Useful Life while simultaneously quantifying prediction uncertainty through model disagreement. Layer~2 used a Deep Q-Network agent that observed the ensemble mean prediction, instantaneous and rolling uncertainty, and an aggregate sensor-health term. The agent was trained with noise augmentation, where seventy per cent of training episodes injected Gaussian sensor noise at varying intensities, teaching the agent that elevated ensemble disagreement signals unreliable predictions. Layer~3 implemented a two-tier safety supervisor that overrode the agent when the ensemble confidently predicted imminent failure, providing a deterministic safety backstop.

The framework was evaluated through two primary 500-episode-per-condition ablation experiments across eight noise levels, plus a derived safety-contribution analysis. When the safety supervisor was removed, the uncertainty-aware agent retained positive reward at heavy noise while a blind control agent, identical but denied the uncertainty signal, collapsed to negative returns. Under the full three-layer system, the safety supervisor reduced catastrophic failures to near zero while the uncertainty-aware agent achieved a 70.6\% autonomous optimal-timing rate on clean data compared to 42.8\% for the blind agent, demonstrating genuine decision-making superiority rather than reliance on the safety net. A financial analysis projected average annual fleet savings of \pounds556,950 for a representative fifty-engine deployment, with savings remaining positive at all eight evaluated noise levels.

This work shows that making a reinforcement learning agent explicitly aware of its own prediction uncertainty can produce more robust and more autonomous maintenance decisions under realistic sensor degradation. The resulting architectural pattern is therefore a plausible basis for other IoT-monitored assets, subject to asset-specific retraining and revalidation.

\chapter{Introduction}

Industrial IoT systems, from aircraft engines and wind turbines to CNC spindles, HVAC compressors, and pump fleets, all share the same core problem: timely maintenance prevents costly failure, but doing it too early wastes useful component life. A single unplanned failure can halt operations for weeks, cost hundreds of thousands of pounds in emergency repair, and in safety-critical domains endanger lives. Organisations have therefore invested heavily in Predictive Maintenance (PdM), systems that predict when an asset will fail so that intervention can be scheduled at the optimal moment. This study develops a PdM \emph{framework} with asset-agnostic interfaces; turbofan engines and the NASA C-MAPSS dataset are used as the validation case study because they provide a demanding and extensively benchmarked instance of the broader IoT problem.


Recent advances in deep learning have made it possible to predict Remaining Useful Life (RUL) with reasonable accuracy by training neural networks on historical sensor data. However, a critical weakness persists in most deployed systems. They produce a single point prediction and treat it as certain, even when the underlying sensor data has been corrupted by noise, drift, or partial failure. In reality, sensors degrade throughout an asset's operational life, and a prediction made from corrupted data deserves far less confidence than one made from clean readings. Ignoring this distinction leads to a dangerous situation: maintenance decisions are made with equal conviction whether the prediction is trustworthy or unreliable.


The work tackled that problem directly. Rather than building only another RUL predictor, it developed a decision-making framework that accounts for when its predictions are unreliable. The system comprised three cooperating layers: a deep learning ensemble that quantified prediction uncertainty, a reinforcement learning agent that learned to interpret that uncertainty signal when choosing whether to wait or perform maintenance, and a deterministic safety supervisor that provided a final safety backstop against late-stage failure. The result was a framework that performed comparably to conventional systems under ideal conditions, but degraded more gracefully when sensor noise was present, the scenario that is most consequential in deployment.


The next sections give the background to predictive maintenance and uncertainty quantification, set out the project aims, explain the methodology, and describe how the rest of the report is organised.

\section{Background}

Predictive maintenance occupies the middle ground between two costly extremes: reactive maintenance, which risks unplanned downtime, and time-based preventive maintenance, which wastes useful component life \citep{si2011remaining}. Industrial IoT sensor networks have made condition data abundant, but turning noisy multivariate streams into reliable maintenance decisions is still largely unsolved.

Aerospace was the natural early adopter. Turbofan engines record dozens of sensor channels per cycle, and NASA's C-MAPSS dataset \citep{saxena2008damage} has become the standard run-to-failure benchmark used in hundreds of subsequent studies.


Data-driven approaches, particularly Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks \citep{hochreiter1997long}, have become dominant for RUL prediction owing to their ability to capture temporal dependencies in sequential sensor data. However, a persistent gap remains between predicting RUL and acting on that prediction: much of the literature focuses on minimising prediction error without considering what a decision-maker should do with an imperfect prediction. Reinforcement learning has been applied to condition-based maintenance planning \citep{zhang2019deep}, and probabilistic RUL estimates have also been connected to deep RL maintenance planning for aircraft engines \citep{lee2023deep}. The work reported here builds on that direction through a specific integration of LSTM deep-ensemble disagreement, noise-augmented DQN training, and a two-tier deterministic supervisor.


Accordingly, the design fed uncertainty from a deep LSTM ensemble directly into a DQN agent's observation space, trained the agent under noisy conditions so that it learned how to respond when predictions became unreliable, and added a deterministic safety supervisor as a final layer of protection. The literature underpinning each component is reviewed in Chapter~2.

\section{Aims and Objectives}
\label{sec:objectives}

The aim was to develop and evaluate a three-layer hybrid artificial intelligence framework that combines uncertainty-aware deep learning, reinforcement learning, and rule-based safety logic for maintenance scheduling under sensor degradation. The interfaces were designed to be asset-agnostic: the observation space (normalised RUL, instantaneous uncertainty, rolling uncertainty, and an aggregate sensor-health term) and the supervisor thresholds do not depend on turbofan-specific features. This report validates the design only on C-MAPSS; transfer to another IoT asset class would still require retraining Layer~1 and revalidating Layers~2 and~3 against that asset's data and failure modes.

\paragraph{Why C-MAPSS?} A general framework still needs a concrete validation case, and C-MAPSS was chosen for four reasons: it is the dominant public run-to-failure benchmark in the prognostics literature \citep{saxena2008damage}; it is challenging enough to stress the design under noisy, multivariate, safety-critical conditions; it provides true RUL labels at every cycle; and it is public, making the work reproducible. Success on C-MAPSS therefore supports the architectural case for analogous run-to-failure settings, but it does not by itself prove transfer to other domains.


Eight objectives were defined:

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{Data pipeline.} Load and preprocess C-MAPSS FD001/FD002, RUL cap 125, sliding windows.
  \item \textbf{LSTM ensemble (Layer~1).} Train five random-subset LSTMs, validate against published baselines, and check that ensemble disagreement tracks prediction error.
  \item \textbf{Gymnasium environment.} Formulate maintenance as an MDP and implement a custom environment exposing prediction, uncertainty, and aggregate sensor health to the agent.
  \item \textbf{UA DQN agent (Layer~2).} Train a DQN under noise augmentation; train a blind ablation agent with $\sigma$ zeroed.
  \item \textbf{Safety supervisor (Layer~3).} Implement a two-tier deterministic override against catastrophic loss.
  \item \textbf{Experimental evaluation.} Ablation across noise intensities with Welch's $t$-test and Cohen's $d$.
  \item \textbf{Financial impact analysis.} Convert simulation outcomes into 50-engine fleet cost estimates.
  \item \textbf{Interactive demonstration.} Streamlit dashboard exposing all three layers with adjustable controls.
\end{enumerate}


The original project proposal is included in Appendix~A, and these objectives are revisited critically in Chapter~5 during the evaluation.

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{Research methodology.} Quantitative experimental design. Independent variable: sensor noise level (eight levels, 0.000--0.175). Dependent variables: episode reward, failure rate, jackpot rate, supervisor interventions. Two agent types (UA and blind) under identical seeds, 500 episodes per condition, Welch's $t$-test ($p < 0.05$), Cohen's $d$.

\textbf{Development methodology.} Iterative bottom-up prototyping. Layer~1 trained and validated first; Layer~2 was built on validated predictions and tuned iteratively; Layer~3 was integrated last as a deterministic module. Each layer depended on the correctness of the one below it.

\textbf{Technology stack.} Python with PyTorch (LSTM), Stable-Baselines3 \citep{raffin2021stable} (DQN), Gymnasium \citep{brockman2016openai} (environment), and Streamlit (dashboard).

\chapter{Literature Review}

This chapter presents a critical review of the literature that underpins the three-layer framework developed in this project. Rather than surveying each topic in isolation, the review is organised thematically to trace the logical progression from predicting engine degradation, through quantifying prediction uncertainty, to making optimal maintenance decisions under that uncertainty, and finally to guaranteeing safety when those decisions are imperfect. Each section evaluates the strengths and limitations of existing approaches and identifies the specific gaps that this project addresses. The chapter concludes by synthesising the key findings into a set of design principles that directly shaped the system architecture described in Chapter~3.

\section{Remaining Useful Life Prediction}

The prediction of Remaining Useful Life is the foundational task upon which any predictive maintenance system is built. This section traces the evolution of RUL prediction from classical statistical methods through to modern deep learning architectures, with a particular focus on the NASA C-MAPSS benchmark that serves as the evaluation standard for this project.

\subsection{The C-MAPSS Benchmark}

The Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS) dataset, released by NASA's Prognostics Center of Excellence, has become the dominant benchmark in the RUL prediction community \citep{saxena2008damage}. The dataset simulates run-to-failure degradation trajectories of turbofan engines, with each engine recording 21 sensor measurements and 3 operational settings at every cycle. Four subsets (FD001 through FD004) provide progressively more challenging conditions: FD001 features a single operating condition and a single fault mode, while FD004 combines six operating conditions with two fault modes. The widespread adoption of C-MAPSS enables meaningful cross-study comparison, though it remains a simulation and therefore cannot fully capture the noise characteristics and failure modes of real-world engines \citep{ramasso2014review}.


A key modelling decision in C-MAPSS research concerns the RUL cap, sometimes called the piece-wise linear degradation assumption. Early in an engine's life, the true RUL value can be very large and the degradation is minimal, making precise prediction both unnecessary and unreliable. \citet{heimes2008recurrent} proposed capping the maximum RUL at a fixed value (typically 125 or 130 cycles), under the assumption that early-life predictions need only communicate that the engine is healthy rather than estimate exact time-to-failure. This convention has been adopted across the field and was followed in this project, with a cap of 125 cycles applied during data preprocessing.

\subsection{Classical and Statistical Approaches}

Before deep learning, RUL prediction relied on physics-based models (Kalman and particle filters \citep{si2011remaining}), discrete-state models (Hidden Markov Models \citep{baruah2005hmms}), and shallow machine learning methods (SVR, Random Forests \citep{louen2013svm}). These approaches required either explicit degradation physics or lacked the ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies, limiting their effectiveness on complex benchmarks such as C-MAPSS.

\subsection{Recurrent Neural Networks and LSTMs}

The introduction of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to RUL prediction marked a significant shift. Standard RNNs could, in principle, model the temporal dependencies in sensor sequences, but they suffered from vanishing and exploding gradient problems that made training on long sequences impractical \citep{bengio1994learning}. The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) architecture, introduced by \citet{hochreiter1997long}, addressed this through gated memory cells that selectively retain or forget information over extended time horizons.


\citet{zheng2017long} and \citet{wu2018remaining} demonstrated that LSTM networks, including deeper variants with attention, achieved strong RUL prediction on C-MAPSS. LSTM was adopted as the prediction backbone because it offers established effectiveness, modest computational cost, and an extensive literature base for comparison.

\subsection{Convolutional and Transformer-Based Approaches}

Convolutional and hybrid CNN-LSTM architectures \citep{li2018remaining, sateesh2016deep} offered computational efficiency through parallelisable convolutions, while Transformer-based models \citep{mo2021multi, zhang2022transformer} captured multi-scale dependencies. For this project, the deciding factors were not a claim that LSTMs are universally superior, but the available data scale, the need to run five ensemble members repeatedly inside an RL loop, and the desire for a stable forecasting backbone within the project compute budget.

\subsection{The Prediction-to-Action Gap}

Nearly all of the approaches above treat RUL prediction as an end in itself, evaluated on RMSE, MAE, or the C-MAPSS scoring function, with the implicit assumption that a better prediction yields a better maintenance decision. In practice this is not true: a prediction of 50 cycles remaining is meaningless without knowing how confident the model is, and two such predictions with different uncertainty levels should lead to different actions. This gap motivates the uncertainty quantification review in the next section and the RL review in Section~2.4.

\section{Uncertainty Quantification in Deep Learning}

A maintenance system must communicate not just when an engine will fail but how much it trusts that prediction. This section reviews the main approaches to uncertainty quantification in neural networks, focusing on deep ensembles.

\subsection{Sources of Uncertainty}

Uncertainty in ML predictions has two sources. Aleatoric uncertainty reflects irreducible noise in the data (sensor measurement noise, manufacturing variability). Epistemic uncertainty reflects model ignorance about poorly-covered regions of input space and can be reduced with more data \citep{der2009aleatory}. Under sensor degradation both matter: the signal contains irreducible noise, and a model trained on clean data will be uncertain on degraded inputs.

\subsection{Bayesian Neural Networks}

Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) place a prior over network weights and compute a posterior, yielding a full predictive distribution \citep{neal2012bayesian}. However, exact inference is intractable at scale, and variational approximations such as Bayes by Backprop \citep{blundell2015weight} introduce approximation error. BNNs remain difficult to scale, limiting their adoption in applied domains.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Dropout}

Monte Carlo (MC) Dropout \citep{gal2016dropout} approximates Bayesian inference by applying dropout at test time across multiple forward passes. While simple, \citet{osband2016risk} showed it tends to underestimate uncertainty in out-of-distribution regions, precisely the scenario that matters when sensor noise corrupts inputs. It also increases inference latency.

\subsection{Deep Ensembles}

\citet{lakshminarayanan2017simple} proposed deep ensembles: train $M$ independently initialised networks on the same or bootstrapped data and use their disagreement as an uncertainty measure. The mean across members gives the point estimate and the standard deviation quantifies uncertainty. When members agree, the input is typically in-distribution; when they disagree, the input is unusual or ambiguous.


Deep ensembles were chosen because they require no architectural modifications, scale straightforwardly, and \citet{ovadia2019can} showed they produce better-calibrated uncertainty than MC Dropout or variational methods, particularly under dataset shift, which is relevant to sensor-degradation stress tests. Random subset sampling introduces diversity that correlates with improved uncertainty estimates \citep{fort2019deep}. The main limitation is linear computational cost, but five forward passes through a two-layer LSTM completed in under 50 milliseconds on commodity hardware, well within the cycle-by-cycle decision loop.

Two further families were considered and not adopted. Full Bayesian neural networks \citep{neal2012bayesian, blundell2015weight} give a posterior over weights but are harder to scale, need specialised inference libraries, and do not produce a clean scalar $\sigma$ for the DQN observation. Conformal prediction \citep{angelopoulos2021gentle} provides distribution-free coverage guarantees but outputs an interval rather than a continuous disagreement score, making it awkward as a single RL observation dimension without extra engineering. Deep ensembles were preferred because they are implementable in plain PyTorch, produce a well-understood scalar signal, and have mature empirical support under shift. BNNs and conformal prediction remain plausible for follow-up work.

\subsection{Uncertainty in Predictive Maintenance}

Uncertainty quantification has seen growing but still uneven application in predictive maintenance. \citet{peng2020bayesian} trained a Bayesian deep-learning prognostics model and explicitly treated prognostic uncertainty, while \citet{lee2023deep} connected probabilistic RUL prognostics to deep RL maintenance planning for aircraft engines. The present study therefore does not claim to be the first uncertainty-aware maintenance decision system. Its contribution is narrower: routing LSTM deep-ensemble disagreement into the DQN observation, reward shaping, and supervisor gate in one reproducible noise-stress-tested prototype.

\section{Reinforcement Learning for Predictive Maintenance}

With a reliable RUL prediction and an uncertainty signal available, the remaining challenge is to make good maintenance decisions. This section reviews RL for maintenance scheduling and the gaps that motivated the project's approach.

\subsection{Maintenance as a Markov Decision Process}

Maintenance scheduling maps naturally onto a Markov Decision Process: an agent observes the engine state, selects an action (typically wait or maintain), and receives a reward reflecting decision quality. The Markov property holds when the observation captures enough information about the degradation state, which is why this project used four dimensions: ensemble mean RUL, instantaneous uncertainty, rolling uncertainty, and aggregate current sensor health.


\citet{zhang2019deep} demonstrated that deep reinforcement learning can be used for condition-based maintenance planning, while \citet{lee2023deep} showed how probabilistic RUL prognostics can feed a DRL maintenance policy for aircraft engines. Those studies motivate the RL formulation used here, but this project makes a different engineering choice: a discrete DQN policy receives explicit deep-ensemble uncertainty features and is evaluated under controlled sensor-noise stress tests.

\subsection{Deep Q-Networks}

The Deep Q-Network (DQN), introduced by \citet{mnih2015human} for Atari, approximates $Q^*(s,a)$ with a neural network, uses experience replay and target networks for stability, and achieved human-level performance on a range of tasks. DQN was selected for this project because value-based methods are a natural fit for small discrete action spaces, replay allows reuse of expensive ensemble-inference transitions, and Stable-Baselines3 \citep{raffin2021stable} provides a well-tested implementation.

\subsection{Alternative RL Approaches}

Policy gradient methods (PPO \citep{schulman2017proximal}, SAC \citep{haarnoja2018soft}) target continuous or high-dimensional action spaces and offer no structural advantage for a discrete two-action problem. Two further paradigms were considered and set aside. Model-based RL could improve sample efficiency via a learned dynamics model, but the C-MAPSS environment already exposes ground-truth trajectories, so this would duplicate work done by the LSTM ensemble and add a second source of model error. Offline RL \citep{levine2020offline} would fit real airline maintenance logs well, but it needs a logged behaviour policy with action-outcome pairs, and C-MAPSS provides only degradation trajectories without maintenance actions. Offline RL is therefore a strong candidate for future work on real data but not feasible on this benchmark. Advanced DQN variants (Double DQN \citep{vanhasselt2016deep}, Duelling DQN \citep{wang2016dueling}, distributional RL such as C51 \citep{bellemare2017distributional}) are discussed in the design chapter alongside the DQN choice.

\subsection{Noise Augmentation and Domain Randomisation}

Training the RL agent under augmented noise was central to making the uncertainty signal usable. This draws on domain randomisation, proposed for sim-to-real transfer in robotics by \citet{tobin2017domain}: training across a wide distribution of environmental conditions produces policies robust to deployment-time conditions.


In this project, zero-mean Gaussian perturbation was used as a controlled sensor-noise stress model rather than as a complete model of real sensor degradation. The choice is consistent with the broader domain-randomisation idea of exposing a policy to varied input conditions during training \citep{tobin2017domain}. During 70\% of training episodes, Gaussian noise was injected into the raw sensor data before ensemble inference, causing ensemble disagreement to rise. The DQN agent thus encountered varying noise levels during training and learned that a high uncertainty observation indicates unreliable predictions that warrant more cautious behaviour. Each ingredient (ensemble uncertainty, noise augmentation, RL for maintenance) has precedent in the literature; the contribution claimed here is the specific integration, not the invention of any single component.

\section{Safety Mechanisms in Autonomous Decision Systems}

Any system that makes high-stakes autonomous decisions needs safeguards against catastrophic failure, especially in aerospace. This section reviews safety approaches for autonomous systems and positions the project's safety supervisor in that context.

\subsection{Safe Reinforcement Learning}

Safe RL seeks to ensure learned policies satisfy constraints \citep{garcia2015comprehensive}. Constrained MDP approaches \citep{tessler2018reward} are theoretically elegant but add complexity and may still violate constraints during training. Shielding approaches provide hard guarantees by interposing a deterministic override; \citet{alshiekh2018safe} formalised this as a safety shield that replaces unsafe actions with safe defaults. The implementation therefore adopts shielding: the safety supervisor overrides the DQN agent when the ensemble confidently predicts imminent failure.

\subsection{Hierarchical Safety Architectures}

DO-178C is an aviation software assurance standard that emphasises traceability, verification, configuration management, and independence in assurance activities \citep{do178c}. It does not prescribe this report's architecture, but it motivates auditable separation between learned components and deterministic intervention logic. \citet{amodei2016concrete} identified robustness to distributional shift as a core AI safety problem. The two-tier safety supervisor addresses this project-level risk directly: even if the ensemble's uncertainty estimate becomes unreliable, the hard critical threshold provides a final line of defence.

\subsection{Uncertainty-Aware Safety}

A growing body of work uses prediction uncertainty to trigger cautious behaviour. \citet{michelmore2020uncertainty} used epistemic uncertainty for human handover in autonomous driving, while \citet{lutjens2019safe} constrained agent actions when a Bayesian network reported high uncertainty during navigation. The system presented here is best understood as a simplified integrated implementation of this broader idea: ensemble uncertainty as an RL observation, noise-augmented training, and a deterministic safety supervisor assembled into one pipeline on a standard benchmark, rather than the invention of a new safety mechanism.


The supervisor here is not a new algorithm but a concrete two-tier design for the maintenance context. The uncertainty-aware tier engages when the ensemble is confident in a low-RUL prediction; the hard-critical tier fires unconditionally at very low RUL. This asymmetry captures the intuition that high uncertainty should make the system less confident in its override decision, not more.

\subsection{Layer 1: LSTM Deep Ensemble}

\paragraph{Backbone choice: LSTM vs Transformer vs 1D-CNN.}
Three backbones were considered before adopting the LSTM. With a 30-cycle window of 24 features, all were viable, but LSTMs remain a strong default for short multivariate sensor sequences \citep{hochreiter1997long, zheng2017long}; Transformers \citep{vaswani2017attention, zhang2022transformer} become more attractive on longer sequences; and 1D-CNNs \citep{li2018remaining} offer efficiency but less explicit temporal memory. On the available hardware, the LSTM ensemble trained in roughly 25 minutes, whereas pilot Transformer runs were materially slower and would have constrained the RL budget. Deep-ensemble uncertainty estimation is also better established for LSTM-style backbones \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple, fort2019deep}. LSTM was therefore a pragmatic choice rather than a claim of universal superiority.

Each of the five networks is a two-layer LSTM (hidden dimension 100, dropout 0.2) with a linear output head that maps the last hidden state to a scalar RUL. Inputs are 30-cycle windows of 24 features; targets are normalised by the 125-cycle cap. Diversity comes from subset sampling: each model trains on a different random 80\% subset without replacement \citep{fort2019deep}. Deep ensembles were chosen over MC Dropout because they are often better calibrated under shift \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple, ovadia2019can}. At inference, each cycle yields a mean and standard deviation:
\begin{equation}
\bar{r}_t = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} r_t^{(m)}, \quad
\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{M-1}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\left(r_t^{(m)}-\bar{r}_t\right)^2}
\end{equation}
The standard deviation was scaled by a factor of 15 (determined empirically during development) to map the raw range of approximately 0.01--0.10 into a range that was informative for the downstream DQN agent.


Figure~\ref{fig:ensemble_error} illustrates the distribution of prediction errors grouped by RUL zone, showing that the ensemble achieves tight error distributions in the critical end-of-life region where maintenance decisions are most consequential.

\subsection{Layer 2: Deep Q-Network Agent}

The decision layer was formulated as a Markov Decision Process and solved using a DQN agent. The MDP components are defined below.


\textbf{State space.} The agent observed a four-dimensional continuous state at each engine cycle:
\begin{equation}
s_t = \begin{bmatrix} \bar{r}_t, \quad \sigma_t, \quad \hat{\sigma}_t, \quad \Delta_t \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{r}_t$ is the ensemble mean normalised RUL prediction, $\sigma_t$ is the instantaneous scaled ensemble standard deviation, $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is a rolling average of $\sigma_t$ over the preceding 3 cycles (providing a smoothed uncertainty trend), and $\Delta_t$ is an aggregate sensor-health term computed as the mean of the current normalised feature vector in the 30-cycle window. For the blind ablation agent, $\sigma_t$ and $\hat{\sigma}_t$ were set to zero, removing all uncertainty information from the observation while keeping the state dimensionality identical.


\textbf{Action space.} $\mathcal{A} = \{0, 1\}$, where action 0 is WAIT (continue operating) and action 1 is MAINTAIN (schedule maintenance immediately, ending the episode).

The binary action space is a deliberate simplification. It keeps the reward structure interpretable, supports a clean UA-versus-blind ablation, and isolates the uncertainty-awareness contribution that this project is trying to measure. It also excludes graduated responses such as inspection, deferred maintenance, and fleet-level coordination, so the findings generalise to binary act-or-wait decisions rather than full maintenance planning.


\textbf{Transition dynamics.} At each step, the environment advanced by one engine cycle. The next sensor readings were drawn from the C-MAPSS dataset, optionally corrupted with Gaussian noise during training episodes (probability 0.7; noise standard deviation sampled uniformly from 0.02 to 0.20). The LSTM ensemble then produced updated predictions and uncertainty estimates. If the true RUL reached zero before the agent chose MAINTAIN, the engine was considered to have crashed.


\textbf{Reward function.} The reward structure, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:reward}, was designed to encode the asymmetric costs of maintenance timing:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Reward function for the predictive maintenance MDP.}
\label{tab:reward}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{2.5cm} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{3cm} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{7cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Action} & \textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Reward} \\
\midrule
MAINTAIN & True RUL $< 20$ & $+500$ \quad (jackpot: optimal timing) \\
MAINTAIN & True RUL $\in [20, 50)$ & $+10$ \quad\;\; (safe but early) \\
MAINTAIN & True RUL $\geq 50$ & $-20$ \quad\;\; (wasteful: too early) \\
\addlinespace
WAIT & True RUL $>$ 25 & $+1$ \quad\;\;\;\, (normal operation) \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

The symmetric $\pm 500$ jackpot/crash pair dominates the discounted WAIT stream, forcing the agent to optimise timing rather than default to early maintenance. Time-pressure shaping below RUL 25 blocks a ``wait forever'' policy. Two uncertainty terms complete the design: an urgency penalty for waiting in the buffer zone under high $\sigma$, and a $+30$ bonus for maintaining in that regime.


\textbf{DQN architecture and training.} The Q-network was a fully connected network with three hidden layers of 256, 256, and 128 neurons. Training used the Adam optimiser with a learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$, a replay buffer of 150,000 transitions, a discount factor $\gamma = 0.99$, and an $\epsilon$-greedy exploration schedule that decayed from 1.0 to 0.03 over 40\% of the 1.5 million training timesteps. To address the class imbalance inherent in the problem (most of an engine's life is spent in the healthy phase where no maintenance decision matters), 60\% of training episodes were initialised near end-of-life, within the last 50 cycles of a randomly selected engine trajectory. This end-of-life bias ensured that the agent encountered a sufficient number of critical decision points during training.


\textbf{Algorithm choice: DQN vs PPO, SAC, and advanced DQN variants.} The RL algorithm was selected on action-space fit, sample efficiency, and implementation maturity. The binary WAIT/MAINTAIN decision is a natural fit for value-based methods \citep{mnih2015human}; PPO \citep{schulman2017proximal} and SAC \citep{haarnoja2018soft} offer no structural advantage in this setting. Experience replay also matters because each transition requires five LSTM forward passes, so reusing data is materially cheaper than on-policy updates. Vanilla DQN was retained as the main baseline because the project needed a clear reference point for the uncertainty-aware observation itself; changing both algorithm and observation together would have weakened the ablation. The supplementary PPO comparison in Appendix~B.4 partly addresses the broader algorithm question by showing DQN produced the safer average policy under the evaluated noise stress. The core DQN target and loss are:
\begin{equation}
y_t = r_t + \gamma \max_{a'}Q_{\theta^-}(s_{t+1}, a'), \qquad
\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}\left[(Q_{\theta}(s_t,a_t)-y_t)^2\right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Layer 3: Safety Supervisor}

The safety supervisor implemented a deterministic two-tier override that operated after the DQN agent proposed an action and before that action was executed in the environment.


\textbf{Tier 1: Hard critical override.} If the ensemble mean prediction $\bar{r}_t < 0.04$ (approximately 5 cycles remaining), the supervisor forced MAINTAIN \emph{unconditionally}, regardless of the uncertainty level. This tier served as a final safety net for scenarios where extreme noise caused the rolling uncertainty to exceed the confidence threshold, which would otherwise silence Tier~2 when it was needed most.


\textbf{Tier 2: Uncertainty-aware override.} If the ensemble mean prediction $\bar{r}_t < 0.12$ (approximately 15 cycles remaining) \emph{and} the rolling uncertainty $\hat{\sigma}_t < 0.55$ (indicating that the ensemble was confident in this low prediction), the supervisor overrode the agent's action to MAINTAIN regardless of what the agent proposed. The uncertainty condition was critical: it prevented the supervisor from firing on noisy dips in the prediction that did not reflect genuine degradation.

The two-tier design encoded an important asymmetry. At moderately low RUL values, the supervisor deferred to the DQN agent when uncertainty was high, trusting that the agent's uncertainty-aware training equipped it to handle ambiguous situations. At extremely low RUL values, no amount of uncertainty justified further waiting, and the hard override provided a deterministic last-resort intervention. This design was directly informed by the shielding literature reviewed in Section~2.4 and by aerospace assurance principles that favour traceable, independently verifiable safety logic \citep{do178c}.

\section{Data Pipeline}

The data pipeline (FR1) loaded and merged C-MAPSS FD001 (100 engines, single operating condition; 20,631 rows) and FD002 (260 engines, six operating conditions; 53,759 rows), yielding 360 run-to-failure trajectories and 74,390 training rows in the local dataset files. FD002 unit IDs were offset by 100 to prevent cross-subset collisions. RUL was capped at 125 cycles following \citet{heimes2008recurrent}, all 24 features were scaled to $[0, 1]$ using MinMaxScaler \citep{pedregosa2011scikit}, and sliding windows of 30 cycles produced 63,590 windows. Each ensemble member then trained on a random 80\% subset without replacement, i.e. 50,872 windows, with the remaining 12,718 windows used for that member's validation split. Full pipeline details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:impl_details}.

\section{LSTM Ensemble Training}

Five LSTM networks (FR2) were trained on random 80\% subsets of the training data (seed = 42 + $i \times 17$), following the diversity strategy of \citet{fort2019deep}. Each model comprised a two-layer LSTM (hidden dimension 100, dropout 0.2) followed by a linear output layer. Training used Adam \citep{kingma2014adam} with learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling, early stopping (patience 15), and gradient clipping at 1.0. Models converged within 40--60 epochs, achieving validation RMSE of 12--15 cycles. At inference time, the ensemble mean served as the point estimate and the standard deviation quantified uncertainty \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple}. Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty_error} shows that ensemble disagreement is positively associated with prediction error, supporting its use as a relative reliability signal \citep{ovadia2019can}. The full training procedure and hyperparameters are documented in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}.

\section{Gymnasium Reinforcement Learning Environment}

The \texttt{PdMEnvironment} class (FR3) extended \texttt{gymnasium.Env} \citep{brockman2016openai} and translated the MDP formulation from Section~3.5.2 into executable code. At each step, a 30-cycle sensor window was passed through the ensemble, and the resulting mean, scaled standard deviation (UNCERTAINTY\_SCALE = 15), rolling uncertainty average, and aggregate current sensor health formed the four-dimensional observation vector.


\textbf{Noise augmentation.} Per-episode noise injection, inspired by domain randomisation \citep{tobin2017domain}, was applied with probability 0.7, sampling noise standard deviation uniformly from $[0.02, 0.20]$. This variable-noise design exposed the agent to a continuous spectrum of degradation intensities during training.


\textbf{End-of-life training bias.} To counter the class imbalance (87\% of random starts fall in the healthy phase), 60\% of training episodes were initialised within the last 50 cycles, ensuring sufficient exposure to critical decision points \citep{lin1992self}.


\textbf{Reward implementation.} The reward function followed Table~\ref{tab:reward}, with additional time-pressure decay below 25 cycles, an uncertainty urgency penalty for waiting under high uncertainty in the buffer zone, and a proactive maintenance bonus of $+30$ for acting under uncertainty near end of life.


\textbf{Blind ablation.} The \texttt{BlindPdMEnvironment} subclass (FR5) zeroed both uncertainty dimensions ($\sigma_t = 0$, $\hat{\sigma}_t = 0$) while preserving state dimensionality, ensuring any performance difference was attributable solely to the uncertainty signal.

\section{DQN Agent Training}

The DQN agent (FR4) used a $[256, 256, 128]$ fully connected Q-network \citep{mnih2015human}, trained for 1.5 million timesteps via Stable-Baselines3 \citep{raffin2021stable} with a 150,000-transition replay buffer, $\gamma = 0.99$, and $\epsilon$-greedy exploration decaying from 1.0 to 0.03 over 40\% of training. The noise augmentation and end-of-life bias exposed the agent to clean, moderate, and heavy noise regimes, teaching it to adapt its policy to the current uncertainty level rather than memorise a fixed mapping. The blind control agent was trained identically but on the \texttt{BlindPdMEnvironment}, which zeroed the uncertainty dimensions. Full hyperparameters are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}.

\section{Safety Supervisor}

The safety supervisor implemented FR6 as a pure Python function that accepted the DQN agent's proposed action and the current observation vector and returned a possibly modified action. The implementation was deliberately kept outside any learned component to provide deterministic and auditable intervention logic, following the shielding principle formalised by \citet{alshiekh2018safe} and the assurance emphasis on traceable verification in aerospace software standards such as DO-178C \citep{do178c}.


\textbf{Two-tier logic.} As specified in Section~3.5.3, the function implemented two override conditions evaluated in priority order. Tier~1 (hard critical) fired unconditionally when the ensemble mean prediction fell below 0.04 (approximately 5 cycles remaining), providing a deterministic last-resort intervention. Tier~2 (uncertainty-aware) fired when the prediction fell below 0.12 (approximately 15 cycles) \emph{and} the rolling uncertainty was below 0.55, ensuring that the override only engaged when the ensemble was confident in its low-RUL assessment. If neither condition was met, the agent's original action passed through unmodified.


\textbf{Interaction with the two agent types.} The design created an asymmetry central to evaluation. Because the blind agent reports rolling uncertainty of zero, it almost always satisfies the confidence condition and is overridden more often, including on some noise-driven low predictions. The UA agent reports true disagreement, so confidence-gated overrides are more selective, preserving autonomy while retaining hard-critical protection.

\section{Implementation Challenges}

Three non-trivial challenges arose during development. First, end-of-life class imbalance caused early agents to learn a ``wait forever'' policy; this was resolved by biasing 60\% of episodes to start within the last 50 cycles. Second, the raw ensemble standard deviation range (0.01--0.10) was too narrow for the DQN; the UNCERTAINTY\_SCALE factor of 15 was calibrated to provide clearer separation across noise regimes. Third, the initial crash penalty of $-100$ was insufficient (discounted present value of only $-20$ at 160 steps); increasing it to $-500$ made crash avoidance materially relevant from early degradation. Extended details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:impl_details}.

\section{Testing}

Testing was conducted at three levels using an automated \texttt{pytest} suite (\texttt{test\_system.py}, 29 test cases, all passing). Unit tests verified individual module correctness (data pipeline, LSTM output shapes, environment API compliance); integration tests validated ensemble-environment interaction, agent loading, and safety supervisor override behaviour; and system-level validation ran the ablation study on a subset of conditions to verify statistical outputs and reproducibility. The full testing protocol and results are documented in Appendix~\ref{app:testing}.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Autonomy and Safety Contribution Analysis}

Experiment~3 computed the delta between Experiments~1 and~2 to quantify each agent's reliance on the safety supervisor. Figure~\ref{fig:exp3_chart} presents the safety contribution analysis.


The autonomy analysis produced the most important qualitative finding of the evaluation. Under the full system on clean data, the UA agent achieved 70.6\% autonomous jackpots (353 of 500) versus 42.8\% (214 of 500) for the blind agent. The gap persisted across noise levels: 133 vs 55 autonomous jackpots at $\sigma = 0.050$, 60 vs 20 at $\sigma = 0.100$.

\paragraph{RL policy or safety net: which is driving success?}
A safety supervisor can easily mask a weak RL policy, so this question has to be confronted directly. For the UA agent, the supervisor fired on 13 episodes out of 500 on clean data (2.6\%), while the agent produced 353 autonomous jackpots before the supervisor had any chance to intervene. The RL policy is doing the work; the supervisor is a backstop. For the blind agent the pattern inverts: the supervisor fired on 201 episodes (40.2\%) with only 214 autonomous jackpots. The blind agent is essentially passive, and most of its apparent safety comes from the shield. Under the UA condition, system success is therefore driven by the RL policy rather than by the safety net.

\subsection{Financial Impact Analysis}
\label{sec:financial_analysis}

The financial analysis translated simulation outcomes into a representative aerospace case-study cost model for a fleet of 50 turbofan engines making 200 maintenance decisions annually (FR8). The cost model assigned realistic industry costs to each outcome category: \pounds18,000 for optimal timing (jackpot), \pounds35,000 for acceptable but early maintenance, \pounds52,000 for wasteful early maintenance, and \pounds275,000 for an unplanned failure event including emergency repair, flight cancellations, and passenger compensation.


Because the full three-layer architecture can force safety overrides, the final cost model also included an override premium for supervisor-forced interventions. This reflects operational reality: even when failure is avoided, reactive supervisor-triggered maintenance is less efficient than agent-initiated planned maintenance due to urgent scheduling and logistics overhead.


Under the full three-layer system, UA produced lower average cost per decision than blind at all eight noise levels: \pounds36,863 versus \pounds39,648 (\pounds2,785, or 7.0\%, saved per decision). Although the percentage reduction is moderate, it was achieved while improving robustness and reducing supervisor dependence, yielding \pounds556,950 annual savings and \pounds2,784,750 over five years for the representative 50-engine fleet. Savings remained positive at all evaluated noise levels.


Under the same linear scaling assumption, this rises to \pounds5.57M over five years for 100 engines and \pounds11.14M for 200 engines; Appendix~B.3 provides the sensitivity table.


The override statistics provide the mechanism behind this result. As noise increased, blind remained more dependent on supervisor intervention (for example, 95\% overrides at $\sigma = 0.175$ versus 83\% for UA), while UA retained greater autonomous control. This autonomy gap translated directly into lower total operating cost once override-driven operational overheads were accounted for.

\subsection{Results Summary}

Five findings emerge from the experiments. (1) UA beat blind on the fair comparison (Experiment~1, no supervisor) at every noise level and maintained positive reward where blind collapsed to $-112.8$. (2) The two-tier supervisor drove failure rates to $\approx$0\% for both agents. (3) The UA agent was substantially more autonomous: 97.4\% autonomy and 70.6\% autonomous jackpots on clean data, against 59.8\% and 42.8\% for blind. (4) The learned DQN outperformed all fixed-threshold baselines, particularly under noise where adaptive timing matters. \section{Evaluation}

This section critically assesses the experimental results against the eight project objectives, evaluates the methodology, acknowledges limitations, and positions the work within the broader research landscape.

\subsection{Evaluation Criteria}

Four quantitative criteria were used: mean episode reward, failure rate, jackpot rate, and statistical significance (Welch's t-test and Cohen's d) \citep{cohen1988statistical}. Two further metrics were introduced: autonomy percentage and autonomous jackpot count, which measure how much the agent's own policy, rather than the safety net, drove performance.


Each metric answered a different question: mean reward for overall utility, failure rate for safety, jackpot rate for timing quality, and significance testing for statistical confidence under heteroscedastic RL outcomes. Autonomy metrics were necessary because supervisory rescue can mask weak policies; override dependence and autonomous jackpots distinguished systems that were safe through learned decision quality from systems that were safe mainly through external correction.

\subsection{Critical Assessment}

While all objectives were achieved, a candid evaluation must acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses.


\textbf{Strengths.} The strongest finding is qualitative as well as quantitative. Under the full system the blind agent achieved a slightly higher clean-data reward (432.1 vs 396.6), but it did so through 201 supervisor rescues against 13 for UA. Stripped of the supervisor (Experiment~1), the blind agent collapsed and the UA agent did not. The UA policy contributes directly to safety; the blind policy relies far more heavily on the supervisor to preserve safety. For deployment that distinction matters: a system that is safer within the learned policy itself has a wider operating envelope than one that depends more heavily on external rescue. The same dynamic explains the conservative bias of the UA agent on clean data (a robustness-optimality trade-off analogous to bias-variance \citep{der2009aleatory}, consistent with domain randomisation findings \citep{tobin2017domain}).


\textbf{Convergence at high noise.} Beyond $\sigma \geq 0.125$ the reward gap narrows ($p = 0.131$, $p = 0.528$) because the supervisor increasingly dominates both agents. This behaviour is consistent with the intended design: the supervisor was explicitly intended to take over when conditions exceed the agents' training envelope.


\textbf{Effect sizes were moderate.} The peak Cohen's $d$ of 0.65 (medium) reflects the binary action space and the symmetric $\pm 500$ shaping, which compresses the achievable reward range. Richer action spaces would almost certainly produce larger uncertainty-awareness gains.



\subsection{Comparison with Existing Work}

Direct head-to-head comparison is limited because few published pipelines combine exactly the same ingredients. Prior work supports individual parts of the design: deep RL for condition-based maintenance planning \citep{zhang2019deep}, probabilistic RUL prognostics linked to DRL maintenance planning \citep{lee2023deep}, uncertainty evaluation under dataset shift \citep{ovadia2019can}, and shielding for safe RL \citep{alshiekh2018safe}. In this work those ideas are brought together in a reproducible C-MAPSS prototype with LSTM deep-ensemble disagreement, noise-augmented DQN training, and a two-tier deterministic supervisor, evaluated with 500 episodes per condition across eight noise levels.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Simulated data.} C-MAPSS \citep{saxena2008damage} is the field standard but does not capture sensor bias drift, dropout, or cross-sensor correlation, which the Gaussian noise model also omits \citep{ramasso2014review}.

\textbf{Binary action space.} WAIT/MAINTAIN is a deliberate simplification (justified in Section~3.5.2). It excludes graduated responses and fleet coordination.

\textbf{Ensemble size.} Five members under compute constraints; larger ensembles are known to be better calibrated \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple} and would likely lift the moderate effect sizes seen here.

\textbf{Single primary RL algorithm.} A supplementary PPO comparison (Appendix~B.4) shows DQN winning on average risk-adjusted reward and failure rate, but PPO was trained for fewer timesteps and performed better on low-noise jackpot rate. A broader multi-algorithm sweep with equal training budgets would strengthen generality.

\textbf{No real-world validation.} The financial projections use industry-representative costs but have not been checked against operator maintenance records.

\textbf{Ensemble calibration.} Appendix~H.1 shows the ensemble is overconfident as an interval estimator (PICP 0.61 at nominal 0.95). The DQN consumes $\sigma$ as a relative signal, so the trained policy is unaffected, but a production deployment should add a recalibration step before exposing $\sigma$ as a probability.

\section{Conclusion}

The experimental evaluation provided strong evidence for the central hypothesis of this project: that making a reinforcement learning agent explicitly aware of its own prediction uncertainty produces more robust and more autonomous maintenance decisions under sensor degradation. The UA agent outperformed the blind agent at six out of eight noise levels in Experiment~1, with the advantage growing systematically from negligible to medium effect sizes as noise increased. Under the full three-layer system, the safety supervisor reduced catastrophic failures to near zero for both agents, while the UA agent achieved substantially higher autonomous decision-making rates, supporting the conclusion that the learned policy depended less heavily on the safety net. The threshold baseline comparison confirmed that the learned policy captured non-trivial timing behaviour, and the financial analysis showed that these technical advantages may also be operationally meaningful: even an average 7.0\% cost reduction corresponds to \pounds556,950 saved per year for a 50-engine fleet, with the same result scaling to multi-million-pound savings for larger operators.


The results also revealed important limitations: the UA agent sacrificed clean-data performance for noise robustness, effect sizes were moderate owing to the constrained action space, and all conclusions are bounded by the simulated nature of the C-MAPSS dataset. These limitations, together with the broader legal, social, ethical, and professional considerations of deploying AI-driven maintenance decisions in aerospace, are examined in Chapter~6.



\chapter{Legal, Social, Ethical and Professional Issues}

Deploying an AI-driven predictive maintenance system in a safety-critical aerospace context raises legal, social, ethical, and professional concerns that extend beyond the technical performance reported in Chapter~5. This chapter examines those issues as they relate to the three-layer uncertainty-aware architecture developed in this project.

\section{Data Protection and Privacy}

The C-MAPSS dataset used in this project is a public NASA benchmark with no personally identifiable information \citep{saxena2008damage}. In production, however, telemetry, fleet identifiers, and maintenance logs may become personal or commercially sensitive data when linked to crews, routes, or operators. Deployment would therefore require GDPR-compliant governance, including a Data Protection Impact Assessment, data minimisation, retention controls, pseudonymisation where feasible, and role-based access controls.

\section{Aerospace Safety Regulation and Certification}

Aviation software that influences maintenance decisions would need to be assessed against certification and assurance processes such as RTCA DO-178C \citep{do178c}. A deployable system would require formal traceability, rigorous testing coverage, independent verification, and controlled configuration management. The current implementation is a research prototype, not certifiable software, and would require redevelopment under a compliant process before operational use.

The EASA AI roadmap additionally emphasises explainability and human oversight. Layer~3 partially addresses this through deterministic, auditable overrides, but the DQN policy itself remains comparatively opaque. Practical deployment would therefore require added explainability tooling and human-in-the-loop procedures.

\section{Liability and Accountability}

A central legal question is who bears liability when an AI system recommends deferring maintenance and the engine subsequently fails. Under current UK product liability law, the manufacturer or deployer of the software could be held liable for damage caused by a defective product. Because the DQN agent makes stochastic decisions influenced by noise-augmented training, establishing a clear causal chain between a particular policy output and a failure event is non-trivial. The two-tier safety supervisor partly mitigates this risk by providing a transparent, rule-based fallback with auditable thresholds. Even so, deployment would require clear contractual allocation of liability between the AI developer, the airline operator, and the maintenance provider.

\section{Social Impact and Workforce Implications}

The automation of maintenance scheduling has direct implications for the skilled workforce currently responsible for these decisions. Maintenance planners and licensed aircraft engineers possess tacit knowledge about fleet behaviour, seasonal patterns, and supply-chain constraints that the current system does not capture. Deploying AI-driven scheduling without meaningful human-in-the-loop involvement risks deskilling the workforce and eroding institutional knowledge over time \citep{amodei2016concrete}. The dashboard interface developed in this project (Section~4.7) was designed to preserve operator visibility into the agent's observations, uncertainty estimates, and override decisions.

There is also a broader public trust dimension. Airline passengers and regulators are unlikely to accept fully autonomous maintenance decisions without extensive evidence of reliability. The results in Chapter~5 showed that the safety supervisor reduced catastrophic failures to near zero across the evaluated noise conditions, but that evidence still comes from 8,000 simulated episodes rather than real flight hours. Building trust would require extended validation, transparent reporting of failure modes, and gradual deployment.

\section{Ethical Considerations in Autonomous Decision-Making}

The ethical dimension of this project centres on the tension between autonomy and safety. The reinforcement learning agent was trained to maximise a reward function that balances maintenance cost against crash risk (Section~4.5). That reward structure inevitably encodes value judgements: the -500 crash penalty versus the -20 wasteful maintenance penalty implies a specific asymmetry between safety and efficiency. In reality, the consequences of a catastrophic engine failure are not commensurable with financial waste alone. Any production reward function would therefore need domain-expert and ethical scrutiny, and the resulting policy should be checked against explicit safety invariants.

The noise-augmented training approach (Section~4.5) raises a related question. Injecting sensor noise during training improves robustness to degraded conditions, but it also reduces clean-data performance (Table~\ref{tab:exp1_results}). For a safety-critical system that trade-off is defensible, but it should be documented transparently.

\section{Professional Standards}

The work was conducted in alignment with the British Computer Society (BCS) Code of Conduct, which requires members to have due regard for public health, privacy, security, and the wellbeing of others. Specifically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Public interest.} The safety supervisor was included precisely to ensure that the system cannot, by design, allow an engine to run to failure without intervention, regardless of the RL agent's decision.
    \item \textbf{Professional competence.} The system was validated through rigorous statistical testing (Welch's $t$-tests, Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, 95\% confidence intervals) rather than relying on anecdotal evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Duty to the profession.} All experiments used publicly available data and open-source libraries \citep{paszke2019pytorch, raffin2021stable}, ensuring reproducibility. No proprietary data or closed-source dependencies were used.
    \item \textbf{Integrity.} Limitations were reported transparently throughout Chapter~5, including the UA agent's clean-data disadvantage and the moderate effect sizes observed.
\end{itemize}

\section{Commercial Deployment Considerations}

If deployed commercially, the system would require integration with fleet management, maintenance scheduling, and spare-parts systems. Data ownership and access rights would also need contractual clarity, because telemetry is often controlled by manufacturers while maintenance records are held by operators.

The architecture is intended to transfer beyond turbofan engines: Layer~1 would be retrained per asset class, while Layers~2 and~3 are built around abstract health and uncertainty signals rather than turbofan-specific features. Even so, broader deployment would require per-platform retraining, recalibration under sensor drift, and continuous compliance checks against evolving certification requirements.

\section{Conclusion}

These issues are integral to the contribution rather than peripheral discussion. Several shaped the architecture from the outset: the transparent supervisor for accountability and certification, the dashboard for human oversight, and noise-augmented training as a deliberate choice favouring resilience over best-case performance. Even so, the gap between this prototype and a deployable system remains substantial: DO-178C certification, liability arrangements, DQN explainability, and integration with real operational data pipelines require work that sits as much in regulation and ethics as in engineering.



\chapter{Conclusion}

\section{Summary of Work}

The central question of the project was whether making a reinforcement learning agent aware of its own prediction uncertainty improves the robustness and autonomy of maintenance decisions under sensor degradation. A three-layer architecture was implemented: an LSTM deep ensemble (Layer~1) providing RUL predictions with a relative uncertainty signal; a DQN agent (Layer~2) observing prediction, uncertainty, and aggregate sensor health; and a deterministic safety supervisor (Layer~3) for critical override. The system was trained on C-MAPSS FD001+FD002 (360 engines) with noise-augmented training, end-of-life episode biasing, shaped rewards ($\pm 500$ crash/jackpot), and two-tier safety thresholds.

\section{Achievement of Objectives}

All eight objectives from Section~\ref{sec:objectives} were met. The detailed mapping of objective to evidence is given in Table~\ref{tab:obj_achievement} (Chapter~5); the headline outcome is that the central hypothesis, that uncertainty awareness improves both robustness to noise and decision-making autonomy, was supported with statistical significance at six of eight noise levels (Cohen's $d$ up to 0.65). The complete numerical data underpinning these claims is in Appendix~\ref{app:experiment_data}, and key source extracts are in Appendix~\ref{app:source_code}.

\section{Key Contributions}

The novelty of this project lies in integration and application rather than in inventing new algorithms. Each building block (LSTM prediction, deep ensembles, DQN, shielding) is established; the contribution is how they were combined, trained, and evaluated as a single safety-critical pipeline.

The primary contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty as observation.} Feeding ensemble disagreement directly into the RL agent's observation space, rather than using it only for post-hoc filtering or exploration bonuses, is a design choice that is not widely reported in the predictive maintenance literature. It enabled the agent to learn a sigma-conditional policy that modulated its behaviour based on prediction confidence.
    \item \textbf{Noise-augmented RL training.} The variable noise injection scheme (70\% of episodes, $\sigma \in [0.02, 0.20]$) created a curriculum that taught the agent what high uncertainty means operationally, producing robust transfer to unseen noise levels.
    \item \textbf{Two-tier safety supervisor.} The combination of an uncertainty-aware override (fires when predictions are confident and low) with a hard-critical fallback (fires unconditionally below 5 cycles) provided defence-in-depth safety that reduced catastrophic failures to near zero across the experimental conditions while preserving substantially more autonomy for the UA agent than for the blind baseline.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive experimental framework.} Two primary 8,000-episode experiments, combined with a derived safety-contribution analysis, Welch's $t$-tests, Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, and 95\% confidence intervals, provided rigorous statistical evidence for the main claims.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}

A few limitations bound how far the findings can be generalised, and they need to be stated openly:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The C-MAPSS dataset, while widely used, is a simulation. Real engine sensors exhibit non-Gaussian noise, intermittent faults, telemetry dropouts, and maintenance-induced distribution shifts that the Gaussian noise injection model does not capture.
    \item The binary action space (wait or maintain) is a simplification. Real maintenance scheduling involves partial inspections, deferred actions, spare-parts availability, and fleet-level coordination that a richer action space would better represent.
    \item The UA agent sacrificed approximately 35 reward points under clean conditions in the full-system evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:exp2_results}), indicating a robustness-optimality trade-off that may not be acceptable in all operational contexts.
    \item Effect sizes were moderate (maximum $d = 0.649$), partly because both agents had access to the same underlying ensemble predictions and differed only in whether they could observe the uncertainty dimension.
    \item The safety case is empirical rather than formally verified: near-zero crash rates were demonstrated across the evaluated episodes, but no formal proof currently guarantees that all reachable states satisfy the desired safety property.
    \item All financial projections were based on simulated outcomes and industry-representative cost figures rather than actual airline maintenance records, contractual maintenance arrangements, or real fleet scheduling constraints.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}

The most credible follow-up directions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-fault generalisation.} Extending the system to C-MAPSS FD003 and FD004 would test whether the uncertainty-aware mechanism generalises beyond single-fault degradation.
    \item \textbf{Richer action spaces.} Adding actions such as inspection, deferred maintenance, or extra sensor requests would better reflect operational workflows.
    \item \textbf{Real sensor data validation.} Testing the system against real operational data would be the most important next step for establishing practical credibility.
    \item \textbf{Extended RL algorithm comparison.} The supplementary PPO comparison (Appendix~B.4) showed a safety trade-off: PPO achieved higher low-noise raw reward and jackpot rate, while DQN produced a lower average failure rate and stronger high-noise risk-adjusted reward. If the action space is expanded, revisiting PPO \citep{schulman2017proximal} or SAC \citep{haarnoja2018soft} would be justified.
    \item \textbf{Cross-domain IoT transfer.} Retraining Layer~1 on a different IoT sensor dataset while retaining Layers~2 and~3 would test the claimed domain-agnostic design.
    \item \textbf{Formal safety verification.} Formal methods could strengthen the certification case discussed in Chapter~6.
\end{itemize}

Appendix~\ref{app:visualisations} presents supplementary figures, and the full testing protocol is documented in Appendix~\ref{app:testing}.

\section{Reflective Statement}

The main lesson I took from this project is that getting the technical pieces to run was only part of the work, and not the most demanding part. The first DQN I trained appeared acceptable under casual evaluation, but 500-episode noise-stress tests showed that this impression was misleading: the policy still crashed engines under conditions that the training distribution had not covered adequately. From that point onward, I stopped trusting small-sample evaluation and began treating effect sizes and confidence intervals as necessary evidence rather than optional extras.

The three layers were not designed all at once. Layer~1 came first because RUL prediction was the natural starting point. Layer~2 followed once it became clear that a prediction on its own does not answer the real question, namely when to act. Layer~3 was added after the agent occasionally made catastrophic decisions in edge cases, making it clear that those risks were better handled through an explicit deterministic safeguard than through further policy tuning alone. Each layer therefore exists in response to a concrete limitation of the previous one, and that iterative logic is the architectural aspect of the project that I value most.

The unresolved question I leave is the gap between a prototype that performs well in simulation and a system that could be trusted on a real monitored asset in operation. The safety supervisor reduces the crash rate to zero across the evaluated simulated episodes, but a regulator would require formal verification rather than statistical evidence alone. Closing that gap would be the most important next step if this work were taken forward.

\section*{A.1 Original Project Aim}

The original project aim was to develop a hybrid AI system that combines deep learning prognostics with reinforcement learning decision-making for predictive maintenance in IoT settings, using the NASA C-MAPSS turbofan dataset as the benchmark validation case. The system was intended to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Predict remaining useful life using an LSTM deep ensemble.
    \item Quantify prediction uncertainty through ensemble disagreement.
    \item Train an RL agent that uses uncertainty information to make robust maintenance decisions.
    \item Provide a safety supervisor layer for deterministic override in critical situations.
    \item Evaluate the system against blind baselines and fixed-threshold heuristics.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{A.2 Revised Scope}

During development, several scope adjustments were made:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset expansion.} The original plan used FD001 only (100 engines, single operating condition). FD002 (260 engines, six operating conditions) was added to increase training diversity and test generalisation.
    \item \textbf{Noise augmentation.} Variable per-episode noise injection was introduced after early experiments showed that the UA agent's advantage was marginal under fixed noise levels. The variable scheme ($\sigma \in [0.02, 0.20]$) with 70\% noisy episodes was important for robust training.
    \item \textbf{End-of-life biasing.} Episode start biasing (60\% of episodes start in the last 50 cycles) was added to address the sparse-reward problem: only 13\% of random episode starts land in the critical decision zone.
    \item \textbf{Hard-critical fallback.} The original safety supervisor used only the uncertainty-aware threshold. A hard-critical override (RUL $< 0.04$, unconditional) was added after discovering that extreme noise could push the rolling sigma above the confidence threshold, silencing the supervisor entirely.
\end{itemize}

\section*{B.4 PPO vs DQN Algorithm Comparison}
\label{sec:ppo_comparison}

To validate that DQN was an appropriate algorithm choice, a PPO agent was trained on the same environment with identical noise augmentation settings and a comparable network architecture ($[256, 256, 128]$). PPO was trained for 200,000 timesteps with standard hyperparameters (learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, clip range 0.2, entropy coefficient 0.01, 2048-step rollouts). The reduced training budget (compared to DQN's 1.5M steps) reflects PPO's different learning dynamics: as an on-policy method, PPO learns from fresh rollouts rather than replaying stored transitions, so direct timestep comparison is not meaningful. Both agents were then evaluated without the safety supervisor across four noise levels with 300 episodes per condition, providing a fair algorithm-level comparison. The results show why DQN was the more appropriate primary algorithm for this safety-focused binary decision problem, while also showing that PPO was competitive on low-noise raw reward and jackpot rate. PPO achieved a higher average raw jackpot rate across the four conditions (60.8\% vs 52.3\%), but it also had a much higher average failure rate (26.5\% vs 5.0\%). On clean data, PPO crashed 6.0\% of engines while DQN achieved a 0.0\% failure rate. At $\sigma = 0.15$, PPO failed 50.7\% of episodes compared to DQN's 14.0\%. PPO therefore learned a more aggressive timing policy: when it guessed correctly it scored more jackpots, but under heavy noise it exposed the system to far more failures. In a safety-critical maintenance context, that trade-off is difficult to justify.

When crash penalties are factored in (risk-adjusted reward = mean reward $-$ failure-rate fraction $\times$ 500), PPO remains ahead at the two lower-noise settings, but DQN wins decisively at $\sigma = 0.10$ and $\sigma = 0.15$ and has the stronger average risk-adjusted reward across the four settings. The jackpot-to-failure ratio further highlights the reliability gap: DQN achieved 219 jackpots per crash on clean data using a conservative denominator for the zero-crash case, compared to PPO's ratio of about 16. DQN's off-policy replay mechanism and target network stabilisation produced a more conservative, safety-aligned policy for the binary maintenance decision.

The PPO comparison is therefore best interpreted as a useful but limited sensitivity check, not as a complete algorithm benchmark. PPO had access to the same $\sigma$ signal and learned useful behaviour at low noise, but the 200k-timestep PPO run collapsed to negative mean reward at $\sigma = 0.15$. DQN was retained as the primary algorithm because it produced the safer average policy under the evaluated noise stress.

\section*{C.1 LSTM Model Architecture}

\begin{verbatim}
class RUL_LSTM(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, num_layers,
                 output_dim=1, dropout=0.2):
        super(RUL_LSTM, self).__init__()
        self.hidden_dim = hidden_dim
        self.num_layers = num_layers
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(
            input_size=input_dim,
            hidden_size=hidden_dim,
            num_layers=num_layers,
            dropout=dropout,
            batch_first=True
        )
        self.fc = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, output_dim)

    def forward(self, x):
        out, _ = self.lstm(x)
        out = self.fc(out[:, -1, :])
        return out.squeeze(-1)
\end{verbatim}

\section*{C.2 Gymnasium Environment Observation}

The four-dimensional observation vector is constructed from the ensemble predictions:

\begin{verbatim}
def _get_observation(self):
    unit_data = self.df[self.df['unit'] == self.current_unit]
    start_idx = self.current_time - WINDOW_SIZE
    end_idx = self.current_time
    features = [c for c in unit_data.columns
                if c not in ['unit', 'time', 'RUL']]
    seq = unit_data.iloc[start_idx:end_idx][features].values.copy()

    if self.episode_noisy:
        noise = np.random.normal(0, self.episode_noise_level,
                                 seq.shape)
        seq = np.clip(seq + noise, 0, 1)

    seq_tensor = torch.FloatTensor(seq).unsqueeze(0).to(DEVICE)
    preds = []
    with torch.no_grad():
        for model in self.models:
            preds.append(model(seq_tensor).item())

    norm_mean = np.clip(np.mean(preds), 0, 1)
    norm_std = np.clip(np.std(preds) * UNCERTAINTY_SCALE, 0, 1)
    sensor_health = np.clip(np.mean(seq[-1, :]), 0, 1)

    self.sigma_history.pop(0)
    self.sigma_history.append(float(norm_std))
    rolling_sigma = float(np.mean(self.sigma_history))

    return np.array([norm_mean, norm_std, rolling_sigma,
                     sensor_health], dtype=np.float32)
\end{verbatim}

\section*{C.3 Safety Supervisor Logic}

\begin{verbatim}
def safety_override(action, obs):
    if action == 0:  # Only override WAIT decisions
        # Tier 1: Hard critical -- always fire at extreme proximity
        if obs[0] < HARD_CRITICAL_RUL_NORM:
            return 1, True

        # Tier 2: Uncertainty-aware -- fire only when confident
        is_confident = obs[2] < SUPERVISOR_SIGMA_THRESHOLD
        if obs[0] < CRITICAL_RUL_NORM and is_confident:
            return 1, True

    return action, False
\end{verbatim}

# Inside step() method of PdMEnvironment
if action == 1:  # MAINTAIN
    sigma = getattr(self, '_current_sigma', 0.0)
    proactive_bonus = 0
    if sigma > 0.5 and current_norm_rul < NORM_BUFFER:
        proactive_bonus = 30

    if current_norm_rul < NORM_REPAIR:    # < 20/125
        reward = 500 + proactive_bonus    # Jackpot
    elif current_norm_rul < NORM_BUFFER:  # < 50/125
        reward = 10 + proactive_bonus     # Safe
    else:
        reward = -20                      # Wasteful
    terminated = True

else:  # WAIT
    self.current_time += 1
    if self.current_time >= self.max_time:
        reward = CRASH_PENALTY  # -500
        terminated = True
    else:
        time_pressure = max(0.0,
            (TIME_PRESSURE_START - current_true_rul) / 10.0)
        sigma = getattr(self, '_current_sigma', 0.0)
        uncertainty_urgency = sigma * max(0.0,
            (TIME_PRESSURE_START - current_true_rul) / 3.0)
        reward = 1.0 - time_pressure - uncertainty_urgency

\section*{F.3 Statistical Validation}

All experimental comparisons used the following statistical protocol:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sample size:} 500 episodes per agent per noise level (1,000 episodes per comparison).
    \item \textbf{Hypothesis test:} Welch's unequal-variance $t$-test (two-tailed).
    \item \textbf{Significance levels:} * ($p < 0.05$), ** ($p < 0.01$), *** ($p < 0.001$).

\chapter{Extended Implementation Details}
\label{app:impl_details}

This appendix sets out the detailed implementation descriptions that support the summary presented in Chapter~4.

\section*{G.1 Data Pipeline Details}

The preprocessing module assigned standard column names (unit ID, cycle number, three operational settings, and 21 sensor measurements) and loaded both FD001 and FD002 files into a single DataFrame. Because both subsets used independent unit numbering starting from 1, FD002 unit IDs were offset by the maximum FD001 unit ID (100) to prevent collisions. Without this offset, the sliding-window generator would have created sequences that incorrectly spanned two different engines at the boundary.

RUL was computed as the difference between each engine's final cycle and the current cycle, capped at 125 following \citet{heimes2008recurrent}. All 24 features were scaled to $[0, 1]$ using MinMaxScaler, with the fitted scaler serialised for consistent inference. No sensors were dropped because FD002's six operating conditions cause all three settings to vary meaningfully. The sliding-window generator extracted windows of $30 \times 24$ features paired with the normalised RUL at the window's final cycle, producing 63,590 total windows. Each LSTM ensemble member trained on 50,872 windows and validated on the remaining 12,718 windows for its random split.

\section*{G.2 LSTM Ensemble Training Details}

Each ensemble member was trained on a random 80\% subset drawn without replacement (seed = 42 + $i \times 17$), with the remaining 20\% serving as validation. This follows \citet{fort2019deep}, who showed that training data diversity combined with random initialisation produces meaningful disagreement. Sampling without replacement kept each member's validation split disjoint from its training split while still creating per-member data diversity.

The architecture (two-layer LSTM, hidden dimension 100, dropout 0.2, linear output) was deliberately kept simple because the primary contribution was uncertainty propagation, not prediction accuracy. Training used Adam ($\text{lr} = 5 \times 10^{-4}$), MSE loss, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling (halving after 5 epochs without improvement), early stopping (patience 15), and gradient clipping at 1.0. Most models converged within 40--60 epochs.

\section*{G.3 Implementation Challenges: Extended Discussion}

\textbf{End-of-life class imbalance.} Finding the correct balance (60\% biased episodes, 50-cycle window) required several iterations. Too little bias left the agent undertrained on critical decisions; too much caused over-triggering in the healthy phase. The final values were determined through systematic sweeps.

\textbf{Uncertainty scale selection.} The UNCERTAINTY\_SCALE factor of 15 was selected empirically so that clean-data uncertainty mapped to approximately 0.15--0.30, moderate noise to 0.50--0.60, and heavy noise to 0.80+, providing clear separation. This scaling directly informed the safety supervisor's confidence threshold (0.55).

\textbf{Crash penalty magnitude.} With $\gamma = 0.99$, the initial $-100$ penalty had a present value of only $-20$ at 160 steps, easily outweighed by accumulated WAIT rewards. Increasing to $-500$ raised the present value to approximately $-100$, making crash avoidance material from early degradation stages.

\section*{G.5 Technical Justification of Model Choices}

This section documents the design rationale requested by the supervisory feedback in a structured manner: forecasting backbone choice (LSTM), uncertainty modelling choice (deep ensembles), control paradigm choice (RL), and algorithm choice within RL (DQN over PPO).

\textbf{Why LSTM for RUL forecasting.} RUL prediction from multivariate sensor telemetry is inherently sequential: useful signal depends not only on current readings but also on temporal evolution, lag effects, and degradation trajectory shape. LSTM was selected because gated recurrent memory handles long-range dependencies more robustly than vanilla RNNs \citep{hochreiter1997long}. On C-MAPSS specifically, LSTM-family models have repeatedly shown strong predictive performance and practical robustness \citep{zheng2017long, wu2018remaining, li2018remaining}. Compared with purely statistical regressors or shallow feed-forward networks, LSTM provides a direct mechanism to learn degradation dynamics from windows of sensor history rather than requiring heavy manual feature engineering.


\textbf{Why not use Transformer/CNN as the primary backbone.} Transformer-based prognostics and convolutional RUL models can be highly accurate \citep{li2018remaining, zhang2022transformer}. However, the project optimised for a three-way balance: (1) stable training on available data, (2) fast repeated inference inside an RL environment, and (3) compatibility with ensemble uncertainty estimation under repeated forward passes. A compact two-layer LSTM provided a better engineering trade-off for this objective than a heavier attention backbone requiring larger optimisation and compute budgets.


\textbf{Why uncertainty modelling was necessary.} Predictive maintenance decisions are safety-relevant; therefore, a point estimate alone is insufficient under sensor degradation. Without uncertainty, the policy cannot distinguish ``confidently low RUL'' from ``possibly low due to noisy input''. This distinction is central in out-of-distribution and shifted-input settings \citep{der2009aleatory, ovadia2019can}. In this project, uncertainty was used as an actionable state variable, not merely a post-hoc confidence display.


\textbf{Why deep ensembles for uncertainty.} Among practical uncertainty methods, deep ensembles were chosen because they are implementation-friendly, parallelisable, and empirically well-calibrated under shift \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple, ovadia2019can}. Bayesian neural networks offer a stronger formal Bayesian interpretation but are heavier to train and tune in deep sequential settings. MC Dropout is lightweight but can under-estimate epistemic uncertainty in difficult regions \citep{gal2016dropout, osband2016risk}. For the objective of feeding uncertainty into downstream RL decisions, ensemble disagreement provided a robust and interpretable signal:
\begin{equation}
\bar{r}_t = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} r_t^{(m)}, \qquad
\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{M-1}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\left(r_t^{(m)}-\bar{r}_t\right)^2}
\end{equation}


\textbf{Why reinforcement learning for maintenance control.} The decision problem is sequential and delayed-reward: acting now changes future state trajectories, and the value of waiting depends on future uncertainty and failure risk. Rule-based thresholding can provide safety but lacks adaptive policy learning across heterogeneous trajectories. RL naturally formulates this as maximising expected discounted return in an MDP:
\begin{equation}
\pi^* = \arg\max_{\pi} \; \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r_t\right]
\end{equation}
This allows direct optimisation of the real objective trade-off between early-maintenance waste and late-maintenance failure risk.


\textbf{Why DQN instead of PPO.} The action space is discrete and binary. In such settings, value-based methods are typically efficient and stable when replay buffers and target networks are used \citep{mnih2015human}. DQN also permits extensive data reuse (off-policy learning), which is valuable when environment rollouts are expensive due to ensemble inference at each step. PPO \citep{schulman2017proximal}, while strong in many continuous-control tasks, is on-policy and therefore discards experience after update epochs, making it less sample-efficient here. For this project scope, DQN provided a better cost-benefit profile for convergence speed, tuning complexity, and reproducibility. The update equations used were:
\begin{equation}
y_t = r_t + \gamma \max_{a'}Q_{\theta^-}(s_{t+1}, a'), \qquad
\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}\left[(Q_{\theta}(s_t,a_t)-y_t)^2\right]
\end{equation}

\section*{G.6 Limitations of the Chosen Design}

Although justified for this project scope, the chosen stack is not universally optimal. \chapter{Empirical Analysis: Calibration, Policy, and Training Dynamics}
\label{app:empirical}

This appendix records three additional empirical analyses requested during supervision. The first examines whether the ensemble's uncertainty estimates are empirically informative (calibration). The second opens up the learned DQN policy to explain \textit{why} the uncertainty-aware (UA) agent behaves differently from the blind agent. The third documents the training dynamics of both layers, including a transparent account of the tuning process.

\section*{H.1 Calibration of Ensemble Uncertainty}
\label{sec:app_calibration}

Calibration is a precondition for the entire architecture to be meaningful. If the ensemble's standard deviation $\sigma$ does not track the true error of the mean prediction, then the DQN's observation vector contains a misleading signal, the safety supervisor's $\sigma$-gate becomes brittle, and the reward gap seen in Experiment~1 could be an artefact rather than a real effect of uncertainty awareness. Checking calibration is therefore not an optional extra, it is a sanity check on the foundation of the system.

\textbf{PICP.} The Prediction Interval Coverage Probability \citep{khosravi2011lower} measures the fraction of ground-truth RUL values that fall inside a predicted interval $[\mu - z\sigma,\; \mu + z\sigma]$. For a Gaussian assumption and a chosen $z$, this interval has a nominal coverage (e.g., $z{=}1.96$ corresponds to $95\%$). A well-calibrated ensemble produces an empirical PICP close to its nominal target. An overconfident ensemble produces intervals that are too narrow, so fewer true values land inside them than the nominal coverage would suggest.

\textbf{Reliability diagram.} The reliability diagram plots empirical coverage on the $y$-axis against nominal coverage on the $x$-axis. Perfect calibration is the diagonal $y = x$. A curve bowing \textit{below} the diagonal indicates overconfidence (intervals too tight); a curve bowing \textit{above} indicates underconfidence. Both PICP bar charts and reliability curves are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:app_calibration}.

\centering

The curve sits consistently below the diagonal across the full coverage range, indicating that the ensemble's predictive intervals are systematically too narrow: it is overconfident. A nominal 95\% interval only captures about 61\% of the sampled RUL values. This is an empirical limitation of this trained ensemble. Prior work shows that uncertainty calibration can degrade under dataset shift \citep{ovadia2019can}, which reinforces the need to treat this project's $\sigma$ as a relative decision signal rather than a calibrated probability.

The practical implication for the rest of the system is that the \textit{absolute} value of $\sigma$ should not be interpreted as a calibrated probability. What the DQN actually consumes, however, is not a calibrated interval but a \textit{relative} signal: the agent learns that high $\sigma$ correlates with unreliable predictions and that low $\sigma$ correlates with reliable ones. The decision boundary analysis in Section~H.2 confirms that this is the structure the agent learns. The supervisor's $\sigma$-gate (\texttt{SUPERVISOR\_SIGMA\_THRESHOLD} $= 0.55$) was tuned empirically against this raw $\sigma$ scale, not against a nominal probability, so the interval overconfidence does not invalidate it. A production deployment would, however, want to apply a recalibration step (isotonic regression or temperature scaling on a held-out split) before exposing $\sigma$ as a probability to downstream components, and this is recorded as a limitation in Chapter~7.

\section*{H.2 Policy Behaviour Under Uncertainty}
\label{sec:app_policy}

Outcome metrics (reward, jackpot rate, crash rate) tell us \textit{that} the UA agent outperforms the blind agent but not \textit{why}. To answer the why, the policy itself needs to be opened up and visualised. The approach taken here is to sweep the two most interpretable dimensions of the DQN observation, the predicted mean RUL $\mu$ and the current ensemble disagreement $\sigma_{\text{now}}$, while holding the other two dimensions ($\sigma_{\text{rolling}}$ and aggregate current sensor health) fixed at representative values. The resulting grid is fed through the trained Q-network and the selected action (or the Q-value margin) is plotted as a heatmap. This produces a direct picture of the learned decision boundary in the $(\mu, \sigma)$ plane.

\textbf{LSTM training.} All five members follow the same qualitative loss trajectory: rapid initial drop over the first 10--15 epochs, then a long tail of smaller improvements until early stopping fires (patience 15, typically around epoch 50 of the 80-epoch budget). The curves are close to each other but not identical, which is the desired behaviour for an ensemble: the random 80\% subsets and the seed offsets produce enough diversity that predictions disagree in difficult regions, while the overall learning dynamic remains stable. If one member had diverged or plateaued much earlier than the others, ensemble disagreement would mostly reflect model pathology rather than genuine epistemic uncertainty. The figure shows that this failure mode did not occur.

\textbf{DQN training.} The reward curve shows the three phases expected of a DQN run with a long $\varepsilon$-greedy schedule. Below the 10k-step replay-start threshold the agent is effectively random and reward is noisy around a low baseline. As $\varepsilon$ decays from $1.0$ to $0.03$ over the first 40\% of training, the agent discovers that maintaining near end-of-life yields the $+500$ jackpot and learns to avoid the $-500$ crash penalty, producing the sharp rise in mean reward. The remaining 60\% of training is stabilisation: the curve oscillates within a tighter band as the replay buffer cycles and the target network synchronises every 1000 steps. Final performance sits in the same range reported by the evaluation runs in Chapter~5, which is the consistency check that the training loop and the evaluation loop agree.

\textbf{Engineering note.} Formal hyperparameter sweeps such as Optuna or population-based training were not used. The DQN hyperparameters are Stable-Baselines3 defaults with targeted manual adjustments informed by inspecting reward curves on short pilot runs (for example, increasing the crash penalty from the initial $-100$ to $-500$ after observing that the discounted present value was being outweighed by accumulated WAIT rewards, as documented in Appendix~G.3). The LSTM hyperparameters follow common C-MAPSS conventions. A proper sweep would likely yield small additional gains, and this is listed as a limitation in Chapter~7. These curves are included to document a training process that was observed, reasoned about, and iteratively adjusted, rather than treated as an opaque routine.

\section*{I.2 Summary of Original Work}

The individual components (LSTM, DQN, Gymnasium, C-MAPSS) are established tools. The contribution of this project lies in \textit{how} they were connected and \textit{what was designed around them}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The uncertainty-aware reward function}: no existing library provides the \texttt{uncertainty\_urgency} shaping term. This formulation was designed, calibrated, and validated as part of this project.
    \item \textbf{The noise augmentation training protocol}: the insight that an RL agent must \textit{experience} uncertainty during training to learn from it is a methodological contribution, not a library feature.
    \item \textbf{The $\sigma$-gated safety supervisor}: standard safety overrides often use fixed thresholds. The two-tier design with confidence gating (backing off when predictions are unreliable) is a project-specific mechanism that was implemented and evaluated here.
    \item \textbf{The end-of-life episode biasing}: addressing the sparse-reward problem by curriculum-style episode sampling is a training design decision specific to this domain.
    \item \textbf{The blind ablation framework}: using an architecturally identical agent with zeroed uncertainty to isolate the exact contribution of $\sigma$-awareness is an experimental design choice, not a library function.
    \item \textbf{The complete integration}: routing ensemble disagreement through observation, reward, and safety override into one coherent pipeline required substantial design effort beyond assembling existing parts.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent As in other engineering disciplines, the originality lies less in manufacturing the constituent tools than in specifying, integrating, and validating them as a functioning system.
